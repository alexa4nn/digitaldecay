Week 4: Web Scraping + Networked Archives

Overview

This week introduces web scraping as both a technical method and a way of thinking about digital archives. We will examine how scraping extracts text, images, and metadata from the web, and how that process reshapes context, authorship, and meaning. Rather than preserving the internet as a whole, scraping produces partial, constructed datasets that function as new archival objects.

Themes

  • extraction
  • accumulation
  • datasets as archives
  • automation of memory

In Class

Key Questions

  • What does it mean to “collect” from the internet?
  • What gets included or excluded in a scraped archive?
  • What context is lost through extraction?
  • Is scraping preservation, transformation, or both?

Activity

Scrape a Micro-Archive

Students will collect a small dataset from one or more public webpages using a simple scraping workflow. This may include links, images, metadata, or sentences filtered through a keyword list. The goal is to build a tiny archive, observe its patterns and gaps, and consider how the method of collection reshapes the material.

Workshop Steps

  • choose 1–5 public source pages
  • define a collection rule
  • scrape links, images, or keyword-matched sentences
  • save the datasets as CSV files
  • combine the datasets into one archive
  • generate a simple webpage to visualize the results

Assignment

Dataset as Artifact

Create a small scraped dataset (20–100 items total) and organize it into a folder, webpage, or visual grid. Your project may include links, images, and/or sentences. Alongside the dataset, write a short reflection (300–500 words) describing what was collected, what was excluded, what context was lost, and how scraping transformed the source material.
