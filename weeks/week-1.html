Week 1: Intro + Digital Erosion + Afterlives

Overview

Introduction to the course and a shared vocabulary for understanding how digital artworks age and circulate. This week establishes the key concepts that run through the entire semester: digital erosion, technological deterioration, post-internet legibility, and infrastructure visibility. We examine how born-digital works change as the platforms, software, and networks that sustain them evolve and how those changes produce new forms of meaning.

Key Concepts

Digital Erosion

Born-digital artworks are shaped by technological environments that constantly change. Software updates, obsolete file formats, and disappearing platforms can leave works partially intact. Rather than stable objects, digital works often survive as fragments.

Artworks as Behaviors

Pip Laurenson argues that time-based and digital artworks are not fixed objects. They function as behaviors that depend on technical infrastructures like software, hardware, and networks. When those infrastructures change, the artwork changes as well. Preservation becomes a question of deciding which behaviors matter, not freezing an object in place.

The Poor Image

Hito Steyerl argues that as images circulate online they lose resolution by being compressed, screenshotted, re-uploaded, copied. These degraded files trade quality for speed, portability, and accessibility. The poorness of the image is not simply a loss but evidence of its journey through technical systems.

Post-Internet Legibility

Many digital works are now encountered through screenshots, cached pages, copies, and compressed images. The artwork may no longer appear in its original form. Instead, it becomes legible through its circulation and transformations – its afterlife.

The Myth of the Immaterial

The cultural imaginary treats the internet as invisible, atmospheric, weightless clouds, streams, torrents. This language conceals real infrastructure: server farms, coltan mines, fibre-optic cables, deep-sea mining. Digital decay is partly a material problem.

Infrastructure Visibility

Technological failures reveal the systems behind digital media. Glitches, compression artifacts, and broken interfaces expose software environments, codecs, network infrastructures, and platform dependencies. Cultural objects remain stable only through ongoing maintenance; without it, their underlying systems become visible.

The Shudder

Ian Rothwell borrows the concept from Adorno: a momentary jolt that disrupts the smooth, immaterial veneer of digital experience. Postinternet artworks function as "images of collapse" cryptograms that briefly make the hidden system visible.

TINA — There Is No Alternative

Rothwell applies Mark Fisher's acronym to the internet: it is now so fully absorbed into daily life that imagining an outside to it has become impossible. An "interminable present" that forecloses alternatives.

Key Questions

  • When does technological decay become aesthetic?
  • What is the difference between an artwork and its afterlife?
  • What do digital failures reveal about the systems behind them?
  • If a digital artwork depends on infrastructure that no longer exists, is the work still the same work?
  • What does it mean to encounter an artwork only through screenshots, cached pages, or degraded copies?

Artworks Discussed

  • Olia Lialina, My Boyfriend Came Back From the War (1996) — HTML frame-based narrative; original browser context now obsolete
  • Eva and Franco Mattes, No Fun (2010), performance on Chatroulette; platform was the medium, now survives only as documentation
  • Rosa Menkman, Glitch Studies works, deliberate introduction of errors to expose compression and encoding systems
  • Hito Steyerl, Strike (2010), 28-second video; striking an LCD screen to reveal its material structure
  • Petra Cortright, VVEBCAM (2007), YouTube webcam video with stock effects; history of deletion and re-upload as a form of digital erosion
  • Jon Rafman, Still Life (Betamale) (2013),found imagery from anonymous image boards assembled as portraiture
  • Jon Rafman, Nine Eyes of Google Street View (2009–) screenshots from Google's mapping infrastructure as found photography; source images periodically overwritten
  • Jennifer Chan, Grey Matter (2014), opaque dark object resisting easy legibility and circulation
  • JODI, jodi.org (1995–), deliberately broken web interfaces with carefully composed code hidden underneath
  • Mark Napier, Shredder (1998), web application that fragments and reassembles any webpage, making HTML structure visible
  • Constant Dullaart, DullTech™ (2015–), fictional tech startup satirizing Silicon Valley aesthetics and platform infrastructure

Assignment

Short Reflection (300–500 words)

Describe a digital artifact you have encountered in a state of decay. This could include a broken website, obsolete software artwork, corrupted image or video, dead hyperlink, or a digital project with missing files. Consider how deterioration changes the way the artifact is experienced and interpreted. What does the decay reveal about the systems the artifact depended on?
