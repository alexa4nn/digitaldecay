Digital Decay & Afterlives: Archiving on the Web

Course Description

With time, born-digital artworks erode as the systems that sustain them deteriorate. Works often survive as fragments: expired dependencies, incompatible formats, and missing documentation. In this class, you will explore digital curation and preservation practices through archival case studies, metadata creation, critical writing, and hands-on building. Throughout the semester, you will develop a small artistic archive on the web: collecting, documenting, and exhibiting fragmented digital materials. All student archives will be linked together as a group digital exhibition.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course you will:

  • Critically analyze the cultural implications of digital decay
  • Develop metadata strategies that document absence, uncertainty, and loss
  • Formulate ethical frameworks for exhibiting incomplete digital works
  • Apply curatorial methodologies to fragmented archives
  • Build a small digital archive viewable on the web

Tools + Software

  • Text editor: VS Code
  • Terminal: default Terminal app (Mac) or Git Bash (Windows)
  • Browser with developer tools
  • Google Colab (for scraping and notebook exercises)
  • Google Teachable Machine (Week 7)
  • Luma AI (Week 8: 3D scanning)
  • Claude or another AI coding assistant (for vibe coding)

Week 1: Intro + Digital Erosion + Afterlives

Introduction to the course and a shared vocabulary for understanding how digital artworks age and circulate. Key concepts: digital erosion, technological deterioration, post-internet legibility, and infrastructure visibility.

Readings: Laurenson, "Authenticity, Change and Loss in the Conservation of Time-Based Media Installations"; Steyerl, Too Much World; Steyerl, "In Defense of the Poor Image"

Assignment: Short reflection (300–500 words) describing a digital artifact encountered in a state of decay.

Week 2: Behaviors, Dependencies + Systems

Digital artworks as behaviors that unfold through technical systems. How software, hardware, operating systems, browsers, and networks shape how a work appears, functions, and changes over time.

Readings: Depocas, Ippolito, and Jones, Permanence Through Change: The Variable Media Approach (Sterling, "Digital Decay," pp. 11–22; Hanhardt, "The Challenge of Variable Media," pp. 7–9); Drucker, "Performative Materiality and Theoretical Approaches to Interface," paras. 13–22

Activity: Dependency mapping: choose a digital artwork and map its software, hardware, platform, and network requirements.

Assignment: Dependency map + short commentary on which systems are most essential and most fragile.

Week 3: Archival Fragmentation + Incomplete Provenance

How artworks survive as fragments, traces, and incomplete records. Meaning is shaped not only by what remains, but by what is absent.

Readings: Post, "Artists as Archivists?"; Appadurai, The Social Life of Things (excerpts); Rinehart and Ippolito, Re-collection (excerpts)

Activity: Reconstruct a work from incomplete documentation. Identify what remains unknowable.

Assignment: Write what remains unknown and why.

Week 4: Web Scraping + Networked Archives

Web scraping as both a technical method and a way of thinking about digital archives. How extraction reshapes context, authorship, and meaning. Scraping produces partial, constructed datasets that function as new archival objects.

Readings: Rothwell, Postinternet Art and Its Afterlives (excerpts); Chang, Alive Internet Theory; Paglen and Crawford, "Excavating AI" (optional)

Activity: Scrape a micro-archive using a Google Colab notebook. Collect links, images, or keyword-matched sentences from public webpages.

Assignment: Dataset as Artifact: create a small scraped dataset (20–100 items), present as folder/webpage/grid, with a short text (300–500 words) on what was collected, what is missing, and how scraping reshaped the material.

Week 5: Metadata for Loss, Failure + Uncertainty

How to describe what is missing, unclear, or unresolved. Metadata as knowledge structure. Introduction to HTML and CSS as tools for structuring and displaying an archive as a webpage.

Readings: Gilliland, "Setting the Stage," Introduction to Metadata; Edwards, Raw Histories (excerpts); Burke, How to Fall Down a Rabbit Hole; Chang and Tjalve, Syllabus for Taking an Internet Walk

Activity: Internet walk + rabbit hole archive. Draft metadata for absent, degraded, and uncertain objects. HTML/CSS workshop: build a simple archive webpage.

Assignment: Archive of Digital Loss: metadata schema with Dublin Core fields plus custom fields for loss, uncertainty, and failed access. Three records (accessible, degraded, trace-only). 400-word reflection.

Week 6: After the Original: Deciding What Persists

What it means to preserve a digital work when no stable original exists. Preservation as a series of decisions about what should persist across changing technical and cultural conditions.

Readings: Colloton, Upstream of Obsolescence; Egan, "Linked.Art and Related Standards for Time-Based Media Descriptive Metadata"; De Kosnik, Rogue Archives (excerpts)

Activity: Archive audit + interface revision: examine your archive as if it were already deteriorating, then revise the HTML/CSS so that loss and instability are visible.

Assignment: Version 2: Rebuild the Archive After Decay: revised archive webpage showing what persists, what is missing, and what has changed.

Week 7: AI, Ghost Files + Speculative Non-Assets

How AI training datasets function as archives: shaped by scraping, classification, and bias. What it means to build an archive of material that a machine learning system could not, would not, or should not produce.

Readings: Rothwell, Postinternet Art and Its Afterlives: Coda: "From Surfing to Scrolling" (excerpts on AI datasets); Crawford and Paglen, "Excavating AI"

Activity: Train a Classifier, Surface the Bias: build an image classifier with Google Teachable Machine using your own scraped material, test its limits, and embed it on your archive site using Claude.

Assignment: Ghost Archive: a speculative or non-existent dataset (10–20 items) that documents something an AI system could not produce. Includes metadata for each item with fields for absence, uncertainty, and refusal. Short text (300–500 words).

Week 8: Exhibition as Research: Displaying the Incomplete

How to exhibit something that is incomplete, uncertain, or partially absent. Students begin developing the interface, aesthetic, and entry point for their archive sites. Introduction to 3D photogrammetry with Luma AI. Each student will 3D scan an object that represents their archive: this becomes the visual icon visitors click to enter the collection.

Readings: Paul, New Media in the White Cube and Beyond

Activity: Exhibition proposal workshop: choose your 3D object, sketch your site (landing page, archive index, record view), define your aesthetic, pair critique.

Assignment: Exhibition Proposal: object statement, site wireframe (3 pages), aesthetic statement (200–300 words), technical plan, and content inventory.

Week 9: Prototype Development

Studio time to build your site, integrate your 3D scan, and code your archive interface. Bring your exported model and all archive materials. Use Claude or another coding assistant to develop your site.

In class: Studio work, one-on-one feedback, troubleshooting.

Deadline: Working site prototype by end of class.

Week 10: Final Presentations

Present your archive site to the class as a live walkthrough: the site is the work. Group critique. The shared index page linking all student archives goes live.

Deliverables: Final archive site with 3D scanned entry point, archive index, individual records with metadata, ghost entries, and aesthetic statement.

Final Project

A web-based archive interface hosting your collected, scraped, classified, and speculative material from the semester. Your site should have its own designed aesthetic and feature a 3D scanned object as its entry point. All student sites will be linked on a shared group index page: a collective exhibition of archives.

The final site should include:

  • A landing page with your 3D scanned object as a clickable icon
  • An archive index displaying your collected items
  • Individual record views with full metadata, including fields for absence, uncertainty, and loss
  • Ghost entries: speculative or missing items visually distinguished from present ones
  • A designed aesthetic that reflects the nature of your collection

Arc of the Course

  • Weeks 1–3: Conceptual foundations: erosion, dependencies, fragmentation
  • Weeks 4–5: Collection methods: scraping, metadata, HTML/CSS
  • Weeks 6–7: Critical frameworks: preservation decisions, AI and bias, ghost archives
  • Weeks 8–10: Building and exhibiting: 3D scanning, site development, group exhibition
