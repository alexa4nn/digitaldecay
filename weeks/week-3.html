Week 3: Archival Fragmentation + Incomplete Provenance

Overview

This week explores how artworks survive as fragments, traces, incomplete records, and distributed forms of documentation. Instead of assuming that archives preserve complete works, we will examine how they often hold only partial evidence: missing files, unstable versions, incomplete histories, and uncertain provenance. The week asks how meaning is shaped not only by what remains, but also by what is absent.

Themes

  • archival fragmentation
  • incomplete provenance
  • partial records as evidence
  • absence as meaning

In Class

  • lecture on fragmented archives and incomplete provenance
  • discussion of artworks that survive through instructions, traces, or documentation
  • case studies in variability, disappearance, and reconstruction
  • begin building your archive site: introduce your collection and name its gaps
  • intro to HTML + handcoding round robin

Key Questions

  • What does an archive preserve when the artwork is incomplete?
  • When does documentation become the primary site of the work?
  • What cannot be known from partial records?
  • How do absence and uncertainty shape interpretation?

Activity

Begin Your Archive Site

Start building the site that will become your final project. This week, create a single HTML page that introduces the collection you plan to archive. Your page should include:

  • a working title for your archive
  • a short description of what you are collecting and why (1-2 paragraphs)
  • a section identifying what is currently missing, lost, or inaccessible in your collection, what gaps exist, and what do you already know you won’t be able to recover?
  • a list of what you would prioritize preserving if you could only save part of this material, and why
  • links to any relevant documentation, records, or sources you have already found (maybe a link to an are.na channel)
  • any questions you have about the collection or the process of archiving it

Think of today’s themes as a lens: your archive will inevitably be incomplete. Rather than treating that as a problem to solve later, start by naming the absences now. What is fragmentary? What provenance can you trace, and where does the trail go cold? What would a future researcher not be able to know from your records alone?

Use basic HTML: headings, paragraphs, lists. Style is not the focus yet. The goal is to get your thinking onto a page that will grow over the rest of the course.

Assignment

What Remains Unknown

Push your in-class page further. Write a short section (150–300 words) on your archive site that addresses what cannot be recovered from the materials available to you, why those gaps matter, and how uncertainty shapes the meaning or status of the works in your collection. Consider the questions from today’s lecture: What is missing? Why? Who or what is responsible for the loss? What systems failed or were never in place?
