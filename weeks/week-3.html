Week 3: Archival Fragmentation + Incomplete Provenance

Overview

This week explores how artworks survive as fragments, traces, incomplete records, and distributed forms of documentation. Instead of assuming that archives preserve complete works, we will examine how they often hold only partial evidence: missing files, unstable versions, incomplete histories, and uncertain provenance. The week asks how meaning is shaped not only by what remains, but also by what is absent.

Themes

  • archival fragmentation
  • incomplete provenance
  • partial records as evidence
  • absence as meaning

Readings

  • Colin Post, Artists as Archivists? Collaborative Research and the Preservation of Artists’ Archives
  • Arjun Appadurai, The Social Life of Things (excerpts)
  • Richard Rinehart and Jon Ippolito, Re-collection: Art, New Media, and Social Memory (selected excerpts)

In Class

  • lecture on fragmented archives and incomplete provenance
  • discussion of artworks that survive through instructions, traces, or documentation
  • case studies in variability, disappearance, and reconstruction
  • workshop on reconstructing a work from incomplete evidence

Key Questions

  • What does an archive preserve when the artwork is incomplete?
  • When does documentation become the primary site of the work?
  • What cannot be known from partial records?
  • How do absence and uncertainty shape interpretation?

Activity

Reconstruct a Work from Fragments

Using only incomplete documentation—screenshots, descriptions, metadata, or partial files—students will attempt to reconstruct a work and identify what remains unknowable. The goal is not perfect recovery, but critical attention to the limits of archival evidence.

Workshop Steps

  • review the fragmentary materials provided
  • identify what can be inferred from the evidence
  • note missing components, uncertain histories, and gaps in provenance
  • discuss how those absences affect interpretation

Assignment

Write What Remains Unknown

Write a short response explaining what cannot be recovered from the available records, why those gaps matter, and how uncertainty changes the meaning or status of the work.
