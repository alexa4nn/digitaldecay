Week 7: AI, Ghost Files + Speculative Non-Assets

Overview

This week shifts from preserving what exists to questioning what was never collected, never labeled, or never seen. We examine how AI training datasets function as a form of archive, one shaped by scraping, classification, and bias, and ask what it means to build an archive of material that a machine learning system could not, would not, or should not produce. Students will train a small image classifier using their own scraped material, test its limits, and begin building a speculative "ghost archive" of absence.

Themes

  • synthetic archives
  • generated images
  • ghost datasets
  • training data as cultural canon
  • classification as decision-making

In Class

  • lecture on AI image generation, training datasets, and bias
  • discussion: how do datasets encode cultural assumptions?
  • examples of artworks engaging with AI systems and their limits
  • hands-on exercise: train a classifier, surface the bias, embed it on your site

Key Questions

  • What would a dataset of things that don't exist look like?
  • What gets excluded when an AI model is trained — and where does that absence go?
  • Can we build archives of what was never collected, never labeled, never seen?
  • How do we document the gap between a prompt and its output?
  • What is the relationship between a ghost archive and the metadata schemas you built in Week 5?

Exercise: Design the Archive Interface in Figma (35 minutes)

Design (35 minutes)

Open Figma and design a new layout for your archive. Your design should make it possible for a visitor to see, at a glance, which items are stable and which are not.

Design at least:

  • a layout for the full archive grid or list view
  • an interaction state for absence or decay (this is the core of the exercise)

The interaction for absence should be something a visitor encounters through using the site. Some possibilities to consider:

  • Items tagged as missing could fade or dissolve on hover, revealing the metadata underneath where the image or content used to be.
  • Degraded items could respond to interaction with visual glitching, shifting, or partial transparency, so that the interface itself performs the instability of the material.
  • Trace-only items could expand on click to show the documentation of what was there, presented differently from items that are still present.
  • Speculative entries could pulse, flicker, or carry a persistent visual uncertainty through dashed borders, lower opacity, or italicized metadata.

You are not limited to these suggestions. The requirement is that the interaction communicates something about the condition of the material. It should feel different to encounter a missing item than a present one.

Export your Figma frames as images. Prepare any assets you want to use: screenshots, thumbnails, placeholder images, icons, your CSS from previous weeks.

Assignment: Build the Interface with Claude

Take your Figma design and build it as a functioning webpage using Claude as your coding collaborator. Upload your exported Figma frames, your assets, and a description of the interactions you designed, and work with Claude to produce the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

What to upload to Claude:

  • your Figma frames (exported as PNG or PDF)
  • any image assets, thumbnails, or placeholder graphics
  • your existing CSS from Weeks 5 and 6 (if you want to maintain continuity)
  • a written description of the interaction you designed for absence or decay, being specific about what should happen and when

A starting prompt you can adapt:

I am building an archive website. Here is my Figma design [attach frames]. Here are my image assets [attach files]. The archive has five categories of items: extant, degraded, missing, trace only, and speculative. Each category should be visually distinct. I designed an interaction where [describe your interaction, for example: missing items fade out on hover to reveal their metadata, or degraded items glitch when you mouse over them]. Help me build this as an HTML page with CSS and vanilla JavaScript. Here is my existing stylesheet from a previous version: [paste or attach CSS].

From there, iterate. Ask Claude to help you refine the interaction, adjust timing, add transitions, or handle edge cases. The goal is a webpage where the visitor's experience of the archive is shaped by the condition of the material in it.
