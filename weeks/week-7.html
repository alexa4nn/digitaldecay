Week 7: AI, Ghost Files + Speculative Non-Assets

Overview

This week shifts from preserving what exists to questioning what was never collected, never labeled, or never seen. We examine how AI training datasets function as a form of archive — one shaped by scraping, classification, and bias — and ask what it means to build an archive of material that a machine learning system could not, would not, or should not produce. Students will train a small image classifier using their own scraped material, test its limits, and begin building a speculative "ghost archive" of absence.

Exercise: Train a Classifier, Surface the Bias

Part 1: Train (30 minutes)

Go to teachablemachine.withgoogle.com and start an Image Project. Create 2–4 classes using images from the archive or dataset you built in earlier weeks. You decide what the categories are — this is the first decision that matters. You are building a taxonomy, just like the ones Crawford and Paglen critique. Upload at least 10–15 images per class. Train the model.

As you build, keep notes on:

  • What categories did you choose and why?
  • What didn't fit into any category?
  • What did you leave out?

Part 2: Break (20 minutes)

Test your trained model with images it has never seen. Try to confuse it. Feed it things that are ambiguous, that sit between your categories, or that come from outside your original dataset entirely. Document at least 5 moments where the model fails, guesses wrong, or shows low confidence. Screenshot or write down what happened.

Ask yourself:

  • When the model is uncertain, what does that uncertainty reveal about how you built the dataset?
  • What is the model confident about that it probably shouldn't be?
  • What would a person see that this model cannot?

Part 3: Embed (30 minutes)

Export your Teachable Machine model. Choose "Export Model" and select TensorFlow.js. Either use the hosted URL Google provides or download the model files to your project folder.

Open Claude (or another coding assistant) and prompt it to help you integrate the model into your existing archive website. Here is a starting prompt you can adapt:

I built a Teachable Machine image classifier with [your number] categories: [list your categories]. My model is hosted at [your URL or file path]. I have an existing archive website built in HTML and CSS. Help me add a page where a visitor can upload an image and the model classifies it. Show the prediction and the confidence score. When confidence is below 60%, display a message that says the model is uncertain. Style it to match this existing CSS: [paste your stylesheet or describe your site's look].

Iterate from there. Ask Claude to help you add:

  • a log of failed or uncertain classifications
  • a "what's missing" field next to every prediction
  • visual markers when the model doesn't know what it's looking at

Assignment: Ghost Archive

Create a "ghost archive" — a speculative or non-existent dataset that documents something an AI system could not, would not, or should not produce.

Your ghost archive should be rooted in what you discovered during today's exercise. Use the failures, refusals, uncertainties, and gaps from your classifier as raw material. What did your model miss? What categories did you not create? What images had no place in your taxonomy?

Your ghost archive should include:

  • a collection of 10–20 items: failed classification results, prompts that produced nothing, speculative records, placeholder images, invented metadata entries, or documentation of absent material
  • metadata for each item, using or adapting the schema you developed in Week 5 — include fields for uncertainty, absence, failed interpretation, or refusal
  • a short text (300–500 words) describing what is in the archive, what is missing, and why
  • presentation as a webpage integrated into your existing archive site, or as a standalone folder or visual grid

Use Claude or another coding tool to help you build the webpage or interface. You can prompt it to generate HTML and CSS that presents your speculative records, styles absent items differently from present ones, or creates visual markers for uncertainty.

Possible approaches:

  • an archive of everything your Teachable Machine model got wrong, presented as a collection with full metadata
  • a dataset of images that should exist online but don't — cultural material that was never digitized, photographed, or scraped
  • speculative catalog entries for files that were deleted, corrupted, or never created
  • a reverse archive: items classified by what they are not rather than what they are
  • metadata records with no corresponding objects — documentation of pure absence
