Week 6: After the Original — Deciding What Persists

Overview

This week asks what it means to preserve a digital work when no stable original exists. Rather than treating preservation as recovery, we will approach it as a series of decisions about what should persist across changing technical and cultural conditions. Students will return to the archives they have already begun building and examine them as unstable systems: incomplete, versioned, and shaped by loss.

Themes

  • preservation as decision-making
  • no fixed original
  • authenticity as constructed
  • archives as changing systems
  • distributed responsibility

In Class

  • lecture on preservation beyond the “original”
  • discussion of versioning, manifestation, and distributed care
  • review of previous archive assignments as unstable systems
  • HTML/CSS workshop: revising the archive interface

Key Questions

  • What does it mean to preserve a work that has no fixed form?
  • What is missing from your archive?
  • What in your archive depends on unstable platforms or systems?
  • How can an interface make decay and uncertainty visible?

Activity

Archive Audit + Interface Revision

Return to the archive you began in earlier weeks and examine it as if it were already deteriorating. Identify what is missing, what no longer works, what depends on external systems, and what remains uncertain or speculative. Then, begin revising your archive interface using HTML and CSS so that these conditions become visible rather than hidden.

Assignment

Version 2: Rebuild the Archive After Decay

Create a revised version of your archive webpage using HTML, CSS, and additional scraped traces. Your updated site should show what persists, what is missing, and what has changed. Incorporate at least one new layer of evidence or trace, and redesign the interface so that loss, instability, or uncertainty are clearly legible within the archive itself.
