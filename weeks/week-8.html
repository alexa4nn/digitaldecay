Week 8: Exhibition as Research — Displaying the Incomplete

Overview

This week shifts from building archives to exhibiting them. The central question is: how do you display something that is incomplete, uncertain, or partially absent? We look at how new media curators have approached this problem, and then you will begin developing the interface, aesthetic, and entry point for your own archive site. Each of you will 3D scan an object that represents your archive and use it as the visual icon visitors click to enter your collection. All sites will be linked together as a group exhibition that will be a shared index of archives, each one a different approach to collecting, classifying, and exhibiting digital material.

Strategies for Exhibiting Absence

  • The Placeholder: a visible marker where something should be: an empty frame, a grayed-out thumbnail, a metadata record with no corresponding file
  • The Trace: evidence of something once present: a screenshot of a dead link, a cached version, a dependency map of software that no longer runs
  • The Narration: text or annotation describing what is missing, telling the visitor what they cannot see
  • The Reconstruction: an approximation, emulation, or speculative re-creation (within reach!)

What You Are Building

Each of you will create an archive interface, on a standalone website that hosts your collected, scraped, classified, and/or "ghost archive" material from the course. Your site should have its own designed aesthetic. Be curious, creative, and don't be afraid to experiment!

Every site will feature a 3D scanned object as its entry point, which visitors click to enter. All sites will be linked together on a shared group index page, where each student's 3D icon leads to their individual archive. The group index becomes a collective artwork: a gallery of ghost archives, incomplete collections, and speculative datasets.

3D Scanning with Luma AI

Photogrammetry builds a 3D model from a series of overlapping photographs. Luma AI uses neural radiance fields to reconstruct objects from phone video, so there is no special hardware needed. You only need a smartphone camera.

Suggestions for objects to scan:

  • Personal artifacts that represent your archive
  • Found objects with historical significance
  • Broken or obsolete technology

What not to scan:

  • Transparent or reflective surfaces
  • Objects with little detail or texture
  • Very small or very large objects
  • Objects that are too flat

Workflow:

  • Choose your object: pick something that represents your archive. It could be literal (a hard drive, a broken device, a found item from your collection) or metaphorical (a found object from a walk outside, a personal artifact, a material stand-in for something digital, something metaphorical).
  • Capture: walk slowly around the object with your phone camera. You will make a virtual box around the object in Luma AI and then scan around 3 levels of your object. Top, middle, and bottom. Keep the object centered.
  • Upload to Luma AI: create a free account at lumalabs.ai. Upload your video. The platform processes it into a 3D model.
  • Export: download the model as a .glb or .obj file for embedding on the web. Low Poly is fine!

The scan will have artifacts and imperfections. It might have some holes, warped surfaces, floating geometry. This fits the themes of the course. Decay is built into the medium.

Embedding 3D on the Web

Once you have your .glb file, you can display it on a webpage. The simplest option is model-viewer, a web component from Google that works with a single HTML tag:

<model-viewer src="your-model.glb" auto-rotate camera-controls></model-viewer>

Other options include Three.js for custom control, Sketchfab for an iframe embed, or Luma AI's own embed code to keep the raw scan look.

Use Claude or another coding assistant to help integrate the model into your site. A starting prompt:

I have a .glb 3D model file. Help me create an HTML page that displays it using model-viewer. When the visitor clicks the model, it should link to my archive site. Style the background dark with minimal text underneath showing the archive title.

Developing Your Site Aesthetic

Your archive site needs a visual identity. Consider:

  • What does your archive feel like? Clinical and organized? Fragmented and unstable? Speculative and dreamlike?
  • How should absence appear? Empty space? Redacted text? Grayed-out elements? Dashed borders?
  • What typography fits? Monospaced suggests a database. Serif suggests a catalog. Handwritten suggests something provisional.
  • What color palette? Warm or cold, bright or muted, high-contrast or washed out?

Pages to design:

  • Landing page: the 3D object as icon, archive title, a one-sentence description
  • Archive index: how items are listed, sorted, or arranged (grid, list, timeline, scatter)
  • Individual record view: how each item and its metadata are displayed, including fields for absence and uncertainty
  • Ghost entries: how speculative or missing items are visually distinguished from present ones

Activity: Exhibition Proposal Workshop

Step 1: Describe what you have so far and what you in a perfect dream world my site would feel like.

Share some of what you collected so far, what you have scraped, what you have yet to scan, upload, etc. What is in your inventory right now? Then, without putting boundaries on yourself, describe how you would like your site to feel to visitors.

Step 2: Post a link to your figma

Post a link to your work in progress Figma Sketch file. Doesn't matter how "done" it seems yet!

Step 3: Define your aesthetic (15 minutes)

Write a short paragraph describing the visual identity of your site. Include: color palette (2–3 colors), typography (1–2 fonts), how missing items will look, and one word that describes the overall feeling.

Step 4: Pair and critique (15 minutes)

Share your sketch and aesthetic statement with a partner. Ask: does the design reflect the nature of the collection? Where is absence visible? What's missing from the plan?
